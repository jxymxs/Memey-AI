What‚Äôs Up with Generation Z? Exploring the Rise in Mental Health Issues Among Young People 

The transition from adolescence to adulthood has always been fraught with challenges, and each generation has faced its share of criticisms from their predecessors. Recently, Generation Z (those born between the late 1990s and early 2010s) has come under scrutiny, particularly regarding mental health. Reports indicate a notable increase in mental health problems among under-25s. But is this rise due to greater awareness and reduced stigma, or is there a deeper issue affecting young people today?

The Data Behind the Concerns

A comprehensive three-year research programme by the Resolution Foundation, funded by the Health Foundation charity, has revealed that one in three 18- to 24-year-olds report symptoms of common mental health issues like depression and anxiety. This is a significant increase from one in four in the year 2000. The research, which involved longitudinal data tracking, highlighted symptoms such as sleep difficulties among young people

.

The study also noted rising prescription rates of antidepressants among young people, increased self-harm incidents, particularly among young adolescent women, and a surge in disability claims linked to mental health issues.

Factors Contributing to Mental Health Issues

                                                                                       

Several potential factors could explain this rise in mental health problems among Generation Z:

Increased Awareness and Reduced Stigma: Greater recognition of mental health issues and reduced stigma around seeking help might contribute to higher reporting rates. People are more willing to acknowledge and address their mental health concerns than in previous generations.

Parenting Styles and Education Systems: Changes in parenting styles, such as more protective and less autonomous approaches, and pressures within the education system might affect young people's resilience and ability to cope with stress.

External Stressors: The modern world presents numerous stressors that previous generations may not have faced to the same extent. The cost of living crisis, the impact of Covid-19 lockdowns, ongoing global conflicts, climate anxiety, and the pervasive influence of social media and smartphones all contribute to a more complex and often more stressful environment.

The Broader Impact

Mental health issues among young people have wider societal implications. Young individuals with mental health problems often find themselves in low-paid, insecure jobs. This can create a vicious cycle where financial instability exacerbates mental health issues.

Moreover, the increasing prevalence of mental health problems predates the Covid-19 pandemic, with a significant rise noted around 2017. This suggests that while the pandemic may have intensified existing issues, it is not the sole cause.

Moving Forward

   

Addressing the mental health crisis among Generation Z requires a multifaceted approach. This includes improving mental health services, promoting mental health awareness, and creating supportive environments both at home and in educational institutions. It also involves tackling broader societal issues such as economic instability and the pervasive impact of social media.

As we continue to understand and address the mental health needs of young people, it is crucial to move beyond stereotypes and recognize the genuine challenges they face. By doing so, we can better support the mental well-being of Generation Z and future generations.


                                                                               Barriers to Accessing Mental Health Care in Kenya

Lack of Awareness and Stigma Surrounding Mental Health in Kenya

There is a significant lack of awareness and understanding about mental health in Kenya, particularly among the general population. The stigma surrounding mental health issues further exacerbates the situation, as it prevents individuals from seeking help and support. Many people in Kenya still view mental health problems as a sign of weakness or personal failure, leading to discrimination and social isolation.

Limited Availability of Mental Health Services in Rural Areas

The majority of Kenya's population resides in rural areas, where access to mental health services is limited. There is a significant shortage of mental health professionals, including psychiatrists, psychologists, and counselors, in rural areas. This imbalance in resource distribution means that individuals living in rural areas have limited access to mental health care, leading to delayed or inadequate treatment. The lack of transportation infrastructure and long distances to mental health facilities further hinder access to care for those living in remote areas.

Solutions for Improving Access to Mental Health Care

To address these barriers and improve access to mental health care in Kenya, it is essential to raise awareness and combat the stigma surrounding mental health. Educating the public about mental health disorders, their causes, and available treatments can help break down misconceptions and encourage individuals to seek help when needed. Additionally, there is a need to increase the availability of mental health services in rural areas through investing in training and recruiting mental health professionals, utilizing telemedicine, and establishing mobile mental health clinics.

                                                                                         Community Support and Outreach Programs for Mental Health

Role of Community Organizations and NGOs in Supporting Mental Health

Community and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) are crucial in supporting mental health in Kenya. These organizations often work directly with the community to identify and address mental health needs. They provide various services, including counseling, support groups, and mental health education. Community organizations and NGOs also advocate for improved mental health policies and resources, helping to reduce the stigma associated with mental health and making it easier for individuals to seek help and support.

Promotion of Mental Health Awareness Through Campaigns and Workshops

Mental health campaigns and workshops are essential in raising awareness and reducing stigma. Through these initiatives, individuals are educated about mental health disorders, their causes, and available treatments. Awareness campaigns promote psychological well-being and resilience-building in the community, while workshops provide a platform for individuals to learn coping strategies and stress management techniques. These activities create a safe space for open discussions about mental health, encouraging individuals to seek help when needed.

                   


Expanding Community Support and Outreach Programs

To further improve access to mental health care in Kenya, it is crucial to continue expanding community support and outreach programs. By leveraging the expertise of community organizations and NGOs and promoting mental health awareness, the barriers to accessing care can be gradually reduced. Additionally, the government and stakeholders must invest in recruiting and training mental health professionals, specifically for rural areas. Through these efforts, Kenya can ensure that individuals in need have access to the quality mental health care they deserve, leading to improved well-being and a more inclusive society.
